After the Civil War and the end of slavery, the southern part of the United States faced a big challenge of how to keep farming without slaves. The sharecropping system was one solution, where farmers who did not own land, usually former slaves or poor white laborers, would work on someone else's land in exchange for a share of the crops. While it may have seemed like a fair deal at first, it often led to a lot of problems for the sharecroppers. This demonstrated that the road to economic independence after slavery was not easy.

How It Worked

In the sharecropping system, landowners would provide the land, seeds, and tools needed, while the sharecroppers did the labor. At harvest time, the sharecroppers would give a pretty significant part of the crops that they grew to the landowner. This would be considered payment for the utilization of the land and equipment. While this system was meant to be a partnership, it mostly favored the landowners, leaving sharecroppers with barely enough to survive. Sharecroppers often had to buy supplies from the landowners at high prices and could fall into debt. If they could not pay off their debt with their share of the crops, they had to keep working to pay it off. This made it extremely difficult to leave the reoccurring cycle of poverty. Furthermore, this allowed landowners to easily gain advantage over sharecroppers, who had little legal protection or rights.

Why It Mattered

Even though they were no longer slaves, the sharecropping system had many African Americans, and a small group of poor white people sticking to the land. It was a system that continued to reinforce racial and economic inequalities in the South. Over time, this contributed to a sense of hopelessness and fueled the Great Migration. The Great Migration had many people leaving the South to find better opportunities in the North and West. Though sharecropping was meant to be a way for former slaves to make a living, it really became a form of economic control and a way to exploit the poor. This sytem lasted for decades, showing that the end of slavery was the beginning of a long fight for real freedom and equality.

The Civil Rights Amendments are three key changes to the United States Constitution 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments that were made during the Reconstruction Era which followed the Civil War. They were created to address the rights of former slaves and make sure everyone in America had basic civil rights. These amendments aimed to correct the wrongs of slavery and pave the way for a more equal society.

The 13th Amendment: Ending Slavery

The 13th Amendment was passed in 1865 and made slavery illegal in the United States. It marked the official end of centuries of people being bought and sold and forced to work against their will. This amendment was viewed as a huge milestone, as it legally abolished slavery throughout the country. This allowed former slaves to begin their journey toward freedom and equality.

The 14th Amendment: Equal Protection and Citizenship

The 14th Amendment, passed in 1868, declared that anyone born or naturalized in the United States is a citizen. This included formerly enslaved people, which granted them citizenship meaning the same rights as everyone else. It also promised that no one would be denied life, liberty, or property without due process of law. This enforced the saying that everyone was entitled to equal protection under the law. This amendment became the legal foundation for many civil rights battles.

The 15th Amendment: Voting Rights

The 15th Amendment was passed in 1870 and dealt with voting rights. It stated that no one could be denied the right to vote because of their race. This was a significant step as it allowed African American men to vote, although it did not include women. Unfortunately, despite this amendment, many tactics were used to keep black people from voting. A couple of these tactics included literacy tests and poll taxes.

How These Amendments Changed America

Together, these Civil Rights Amendments were meant to make America more equal, especially dedicated towards former slaves. While they were essential for the advancement of civil rights, it marked only the beginning of a longer journey ahead. The south did not like these changes and resisted them by pushing efforts such as creating black codes to restrict the rights of African Americans. This led to more struggles and a longer fight for equality. While these amendments were crucial, it took decades of activism and additional laws to fully push the agenda at hand. These amendments still matter today as they remind us of the challenges America has faced and the progress that has been made since. Furthermore, they also show that the journey toward true equality is ongoing.

Sharecropping was a farming system that was brought about in the southern part of the United States after the Civil War, during the Reconstruction Era. It was a way for landowners, who were often former plantation owners, to allow their land to produce crops without hiring workers. Even more, it allowed for tenant farmers, former slaves, to earn a living without being formally employed. In this system of sharecropping, the landowners provided the land, seeds, and tools, while the sharecroppers did the labor work of planting, growing, and harvesting the crops. In return, the sharecroppers gave a large portion of their crops to the landowners as payment.

The Reality of Sharecropping

At first, sharecropping seemed like a fair arrangement as it offered the opportunity for freed slaves and poor whites to make a living. It even allowed landowners to keep their farms running. But in reality, it trapped sharecroppers in a cycle of debt and poverty. Since the sharecroppers did not own the land, they had little control over their work. They relied on the landowners for seeds, tools, and other supplies, which would be fairly expensive. Landowners could set high prices for these supplies and could charge high interest rates for credit, which meant sharecroppers often owed more than they earned. Most contracts between landowners and sharecroppers were unfairly biased toward the landowners, letting them hold a lot of power. If sharecroppers did not meet the landowner's expectations or if they fell behind on their debts, they could be kicked off the land. This made it hard for sharecroppers to drastically change their lives or escape poverty.

The Impact of Sharecropping

Sharecropping became one of the symbols of the struggles that followed the Civil War. It kept many African Americans and poor whites in a state of economic dependency, with little chance to move up in the world. Additionally, it contributed to racial segregation in the south, with sharecropping farms often being separate and unequal. This system lasted till the 20th century, reinforcing racial and economic inequalities. Because sharecroppers had so little opportunities to improve their living situation, many decided to leave the South during the Great Migration. They fled to the North and West in search of better jobs and a chance for a better life. Sharecropping remains as a constant reminder that even though slavery had ended, true freedom and equality were still a long way off. It demonstrates that the fight for economic justice and civil rights was a journey that took time and persistence.

The Reconstruction Acts were a series of laws that was passed by the U.S. Congress between 1867 and 1868. These acts defined the process for reintegrating southern states into the Union after the Civil War. They aimed to bring the Southern states back while also ensuring that the rights of newly freed slaves were protected. The Reconstruction Acts took a more serious approach compared to previous policies. It utilized the emphasize of military oversight and stricter requirements for rejoining the Union.

Key Aspects: Military Oversight and New State Constitutions

The First Reconstruction Act was passed in March of 1867, and aimed to divide the former confederate states into five different military districts. Each of these districts were to be overseen by a union general. This military presence was established in order to maintain order and ensure that southern states complied with the new policies following the reconstruction. The act required southern states to write new constitutions that gave African American men the right to vote. It even had them uphold the principles of the 14th Amendment, which granted citizenship to all people born in the United States, including former slaves. The following Reconstruction Acts added more conditions for southern states to qualify to allow them to rejoin the Union. They had to ratify the 14th Amendment and extend voting rights to black men. This aimed to prevent states from enacting Black Codes, which were laws that restricted the freedoms of African Americans. These acts showed that congress was taking a tougher stance on reconstruction, while also trying to rebuild the south in a way that promoted civil rights and equal treatment under the law.

Challenges and Resistance

The new rules set by the Reconstruction Acts faced significant resistance in the south. Many white southerners did not like the idea of military rule or giving African Americans the right to vote. Some resorted to violence, which led to the rise of groups such as the Ku Klux Klan, who utilized fear tactics to maintain control. This resistance made it challenging for the Reconstruction Acts to have their intended impact. Despite the initial goals of the Reconstruction Acts, the resistance they faced contributed to the decline of the Reconstruction era by the mid 1870s. These acts played a crucial role in shaping the course of reconstruction, but their effectiveness depended on actually enforcing them. While they had some success in promoting civil rights, the struggle for racial equality in the United States continued.

Jim Crow laws were state and local laws in the Southern United States that enforced racial segregation and discrimination from the late 1800s to the mid 1900s. These laws were named after a character from racist minstrel shows. They established a separate but equal system, but when put in place, led to significant discrimination and inequality for African Americans. Jim Crow laws affected pretty much every place of life in the South, reinforcing a system of racial hierarchy.

Segregation and Discrimination

Jim Crow laws meant that African Americans and white Americans had to use separate public facilities. This included schools, buses, trains, theaters, restaurants, and even water fountains. Although the laws claimed to be separate but equal, the facilities for black people were always worse. They were either underfunded, in poor condition, or neglected altogether. In addition to public segregation, these laws also made it much harder for African Americans to vote. This was done through the utilization of a series of tests which included literacy tests, poll taxes, and other barriers designed to keep them out of voting. These laws created an environment where racism was not only accepted but legally enforced. Black people faced discrimination in housing and employment, and they had limited legal rights.

Fighting Jim Crow and the Legacy It Left Behind

Despite the harsh realities of Jim Crow, resistance to these laws grew during the 20th century. Many of these efforts led to significant civil rights achievements. The supreme court decision in Brown vs Board of Education in 1954 declared that segregated schools were unconstitutional. This began the upcoming of Civil Rights Movement. This movement led to the Civil Rights Act of 1964, which banned racial discrimination, and the Voting Rights Act of 1965, which protected voting rights for African Americans. Although these legal victories undermined the Jim Crow system, the legacy and history that these laws left behind still effect American society. The decades of segregation and discrimination developed inequalities that continue to impact education, employment, and the criminal justice system.
